Popular factoids about the English language often leave me feeling contrarian,
as a native speaker of a variety of English often not included in these
broad, unqualified statements. "English has no second person plural"? What do
[yous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category:Second-person_plural_pronouns_in_English){:target=‚Äù_blank‚Äù} mean? And the

[popular](https://xkcd.com/2907/){:target="_blank"}: "schwa is the most common vowel in English".

This last statement has often left me puzzled, as I've never been able to
confidently perceive this sound in my own speech. Is this a lack of skill, a
misunderstanding, or is my phonemic intuition pointing at some truth about my
English that can make me feel superiorly contrarian once again?

## Common transcriptions of schwa <span class=ipa>/…ô/</span>

There are a few places where SSBE and many American accents[^6] have schwa.
Making reference to Wells' [lexical
sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lexical_set){:target="_blank"}:

* COMMA: words that end with an open syllable (i.e. with a vowel) are
  transcribed as ending in schwa in both
  [SSBE](http://seas3.elte.hu/cube/index.pl?s=comma){:target="_blank"} and in
  [General American](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/comma){:target="_blank"}, e.g.
  <span class=ipa>/k…îm…ô/</span> (SSBE) and <span class=ipa>/k…ëm…ô/</span> (Am)

* NURSE: stressed syllables with Middle English <span class=ipa>/i/, /…õ/,
  /u/</span> followed by <span class=ipa>/r/</span>. E.g. <span
  class=ipa>/n…ôÀês/</span> (SSBE) and <span class=ipa>/n…ôrs/</span> (Am).

* In [weak](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=EaXYas58_kc){:target="_blank"} (unstressed) forms of
  certain common single syllable words like "the", "a", "at".

* In many unstressed syllables, for example in these SSBE
  transcriptions[^7]: "villain" <span class=ipa>/v…™l…ôn/</span>; "patent" <span
  class=ipa>/pat…ônt/</span>; "synthesis" <span class=ipa>/s…™nŒ∏…ôs…™s/</span>.

* Some accents of English (including many Americans, and perhaps
  increasingly speakers whose speech could be described as SSBE) use schwa in
  even more unstressed syllables where older southern English accents would
  have <span class=ipa>/…™/</span>, for example "private" as <span
  class=ipa>/pra…™v…ôt/</span>

* Some accents of English (including many
  [Americans](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wt66Je3o0Qg){:target="_blank"}, but also some
  northwest English and Welsh accents) use schwa for the STRUT lexical set
  (e.g. "gun", "love", "much" "blood").

So what about my English? Intuitively I can say:

* My COMMA vowel is far too open to be schwa

* Like all Scottish people I know, the NURSE vowels didn't all
  [merge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English-language_vowel_changes_before_historic_/r/#Nurse_mergers){:target="_blank"}
  for me. I think everyone I know (biased to the central belt[^8]) has <span
  class=ipa>/f år/</span>[^9] for "fir" and "fur". I say <span
  class=ipa>/f…õrn/</span> for "fern", often realised <span
  class=ipa>[f…õ…æ…™ÃÜn]</span>. Similarly I say "world" as <span
  class=ipa>[w å…æ åÃÜ…´d]</span>[^10].

* I'm less sure about the remaining examples, but some of them I would
  identify with my KIT vowel rather than schwa.

So, is schwa the most common vowel in my English? It certainly doesn't seem
like it.  In my reading of ["Comma Gets a
Cure"](https://www.dialectsarchive.com/comma-gets-a-cure){:target="_blank"}, I think the only
word I use a schwa in is in the second syllable of "millionaire". (See this
footnote[^12].) One incidence in the entire passage doesn't sound like much to
me! I think my having schwa only in some places in one of the many categories
of syllables where schwa is usually pronounced results in this relatively rare
incidence. Perhaps <span class=ipa>/…™/</span> is my most common vowel, on
account of my using it for weak forms.

## Appendix A: How mid-central is the vowel I've labelled "schwa"?

Something I haven't pointed out elsewhere in this post is that phonetic symbols
and names like "schwa" have somewhat ambiguous and highly contextual meanings.

Take my KIT vowel, for example. I've seen [vowel
diagrams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scottish_English#/media/File:Scottish_English_monophthongs_chart.svg){:target="_blank"}
for Scottish English that put the KIT vowel much closer to schwa than the IPA
chart shows <span class=ipa>[…™]</span>. Similarly
[this](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Received_Pronunciation#/media/File:Modern_General_British_monophthong_chart.svg){:target=‚Äù_blank‚Äù}
"modern RP" vowel chart has a slightly lowered vowel.

Here they are superimposed, with the IPA's reference vowels shown in pink:

![I-vowel-comparison](/images/analysing-my-own-speech/I-vowel-comparison.svg)

I'm not sure if this is an accurate plot of where my KIT vowel is, but it seems
plausible.

There's an implicit assumption in my blog post that this difference between the
IPA's definition of <span class=ipa>[…™]</span> and my realisation of <span
class=ipa>/…™/</span> is irrelevant to our discussions.

In a similar vein, when people repeat the meme of "schwa is the most common
vowel in English", it can be unclear exactly what vowel they mean, and whether
they are even aware of what they mean[^14]. Do they mean any unstressed reduced
vowel in their English? Specifically something in the near vicinity of the
mid-central vowel on the IPA chart? Or just any sound they've seen transcribed
as <span class=ipa>/…ô/</span>?

Anyway, here is me attempting a perfectly mid-central unrounded vowel, followed by my
KIT vowel:

I think there's an idea that because the tongue position for schwa is
"neutral", i.e. the tongue is at the mid-point of both the open-closed and
front-back axes, that it's natural for speakers to default to it when
reaching for what to say next.

I think what's natural is of course dependent on your native language(s). Take
a look at these vowel diagrams for
[Arabic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Arabic_phonology#/media/File:Arabic_vowels_(Monophthongs).png){:target="_blank"}
(three vowels),
[Japanese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_phonology#/media/File:Japanese_vowel_chart_II.svg){:target="_blank"} (five vowels), and
[Italian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italian_phonology#/media/File:Italian_vowel_chart.svg){:target="_blank"}
(seven vowels)[^15].

None of these show a schwa vowel. In Japanese it's most common to hear
„Åà„Åà„Å£„Å®<span class=ipa>[eÃûÀêtÃötoÃû]</span>[^16]. The two vowels used there are both
central, though they are front and back, respectively. My hesitation vowel is
also mid. Is there something cross-linguistic at play here?

A brief look at this [Reddit
thread](https://www.reddit.com/r/languagelearning/comments/mbvn63/are_hesitation_noises_different_in_various/){:target="_blank"}
suggests some variation, but a lot of languages are listed as having mid vowel
sounds, if I'm interpreting the crude transcription correctly: German, Kyrgyz,
Finnish, Spanish. Notable exceptions appear to be Mandarin, Korean and
Polish. This is quite a crude source of information however.
